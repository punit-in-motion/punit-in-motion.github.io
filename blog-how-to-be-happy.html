Lasting Happiness Is Possible, If You Know What To Do

By Punit Shah October 2025

I used to wonder why I, of all people, continued to find myself happy despite the setbacks in my life. Hemiplegia. Broken bones and six surgeries. Picked on as a kid. Dumped on by others. Overlooked and underestimated by those with status.

It was weird. You'd think I'd break at some point. That's when I started researching what might be going on.

What I Found In The Research

Turns out there's decades of research on happiness. Not the fake "think positive" stuff. Real science on what makes people genuinely happier over time.

Martin Seligman at UPenn started positive psychology in the 90s by asking: instead of studying depression and anxiety, what if we studied what makes people thrive? His research found something simple. People who deliberately notice good things in their day are happier.

I realized I'd been doing this without knowing it. Even with hemiplegia affecting my left side, I'd notice when I successfully tied my shoes. When someone held a door. When I made someone laugh. Small stuff. But I paid attention to it.

Most people do the opposite. They catalog everything that went wrong. The traffic. The rude email. The thing they forgot. I did that too, but I also kept track of what went right.

Naming What You Feel

Lisa Feldman Barrett's research on emotions surprised me. She found that people who can precisely name their emotions handle them better.

When I'd say "I feel bad," nothing changed. But when I said "I'm frustrated that my left hand won't cooperate and I'm anxious about looking incompetent," I could actually do something about it. The specificity mattered.

It's not about having a big vocabulary for emotions. It's about stopping for a second and figuring out what you actually feel instead of just saying "fine" or "stressed."

Getting Excited For Other People

Shelly Gable at UC Santa Barbara found something that explained why I had good friendships despite everything. How you respond to people's good news matters MORE than how you respond to their bad news.

When someone told me good news, I got genuinely excited. Even when I was dealing with my own stuff. My friend got into grad school? I wanted to hear every detail. My coworker's kid won an award? Tell me about it.

I noticed other people would say "nice" and change the subject. Or make it about themselves. I didn't do that. Maybe because I knew what it felt like to have your wins ignored. But I'd celevrate other people's wins like they were my own. In fact friends would call me just to share good news because they knew I'd be excited.

Being Less Of A Jerk To Myself

Kristin Neff's research on self-compassion hit different. I was brutal with myself. Drop something with my left hand? "You're useless." Stumble in public? "Everyone's staring at you, you idiot."

I would never talk to a friend that way. Never. But I talked to myself like that constantly.

Neff's research shows self-compassion isn't about lowering standards. It's about not being cruel to yourself when things go wrong. Turns out it actually helps you improve faster. The internal criticism wasn't helping. It was just making everything harder. You can imagine growing up disabled in a world built for able-bodied people didn't exactly help my self-esteem. I had to find something else. For me, I always backed into kindness. I knew I was going to be kind. And I actively left situations where I forced to not be myself. So the stumble or the fall didnt matter. I was still a good person, that part never changed.

What I Was Accidentally Doing Right

Carol Ryff's research on eudaimonic wellbeing explained something about my life. She found that lasting happiness comes from living according to your values, not just feeling good.

I cared about kindness because I knew what it felt like to be treated like you don't matter. I cared about helping people because I'd needed help. Those weren't abstract values. They were real to me. And when I lived according to them, even on hard days, I felt okay.

Pizza and Netflix and TACO BELL (yum) felt good temporarily. But working on something that mattered to me felt different. Better. More sustainable. So good that I'd forget to eat sometimes.

Needing People Around

James Coan's brain research at UVA made sense of something I already knew. When you're stressed and alone, your brain works overtime. When someone you trust is with you, your brain calms down. Measurably.

I couldn't do everything alone physically. But even beyond that, I just felt better around people. Not performing for them. Just being around them. Making them laugh or smile. That was my joy.

When I isolated myself, everything got harder. When I texted someone or sat in a room with friends, even quietly, my brain relaxed. I even started my last business just to work with my friends. Because, despite the job offers, I wanted to be around people I cared about.

Actually Enjoying Good Moments

Robert Emmons and Sonja Lyubomirsky's research on gratitude showed me why I did something weird. When something good happened, I'd pause. Really take it in. Feel it.

Made someone laugh? I'd sit with that feeling for a minute. Had a good conversation? I'd replay it later. Accomplished something despite my limitations? I'd let myself feel proud. I'd dance foolishly, sing off key, and just be silly.

Most people rush past good moments, already worrying about what's next. I learned not to do that. Maybe because I knew good moments mattered. And they were rare. But by savoring them, I got more out of them.

Doing What I Actually Wanted

Deci and Ryan's Self-Determination Theory broke down motivation into three things: autonomy (you chose it), mastery (you're getting better), and connection (it matters to others).

I stopped trying to do things just because I thought I should. Started asking: do I actually want this? Does this align with who I am? Is this what I'm REALLY REALLY good at?

Turned out I was chasing alot of stuff that didn't matter to me. When I dropped those and focused on what I cared about, I had more energy. more joy, more me.

Why I Stayed Happy

There's this thing called hedonic adaptation. You adapt to everything. New apartment, new relationship, new achievement. Two weeks of happiness, then back to baseline.

I never relied on big wins to be happy. Because I didn't have many. Instead, I built daily practices. Notice good things. Be kind to myself. Connect with people. Do work that mattered. Savor small moments.

Those daily practices created a baseline of happiness that wasn't dependent on everything going right. Which was good, because everything rarely went right.

What This Means For You

You don't need to have hemiplegia or face setbacks to benefit from this research. You just need to understand that lasting happiness comes from daily practices, not circumstances.

Pick one thing. Notice good moments. Name your emotions specifically. Get excited for other people. Be kinder to yourself. Connect with people. Do things that matter to you. Savor wins.

How to Be Happy (or Happier)

By Punit Shah October 2025

I used to think happiness was about getting the right job, the right relationship, the right apartment. And then I'd get those things and feel...the same. Maybe happy for a week or two, then back to baseline.

Turns out, there's actual science behind why that happens. And more importantly, what actually works instead. After reading way too many psychology papers, here's what I've learned about building the kind of happiness that sticks around.

Focus on What's Going Right

Martin Seligman at UPenn basically started this whole field by asking: what if we stopped only studying what's broken and started studying what works? Radical idea in the 1990s, apparently.

His research found that people who deliberately notice good things are happier. Not groundbreaking, except it actually is. Most of us spend all day cataloging what went wrong. The meeting that sucked. The email that annoyed us. That thing we forgot to do.

What if you spent even five minutes noticing what went well? I started keeping a list on my phone. Some days it's "had a good sandwich." Some days it's bigger stuff. Doesn't matter. The act of looking for good things changes how you see your day.

Name the Feeling

Lisa Feldman Barrett has this whole theory about how naming your emotions precisely helps you handle them better. She calls it emotional granularity, which sounds fancy but really just means: stop saying "I feel bad" and figure out if you're actually anxious, or disappointed, or frustrated, or overwhelmed.

I thought this was BS until I tried it. Turns out when you can name exactly what you're feeling, it's way easier to deal with. "I feel bad" gives you nothing to work with. "I'm anxious about the presentation and frustrated that I'm behind on prep" gives you something concrete.

You don't need a feelings wheel (though those can help). Just get more specific when someone asks how you are. Skip "fine" and try actually describing it.

Actually Celebrate Other People's Wins

Here's something wild: Shelly Gable's research shows that how you respond when good things happen to people matters MORE for the relationship than how you respond when bad things happen.

Think about it. Your friend tells you they got a promotion. You can say "That's great!" and change the subject. Or you can match their energy, ask questions, get excited with them. The second one makes both of you happier and strengthens the friendship.

I notice this all the time now. How many times someone shares good news and the other person immediately makes it about themselves or downplays it? Don't be that person.

Talk to Yourself Like a Friend

Kristin Neff's work on self-compassion changed how I think about mistakes. Most people are WAY harsher on themselves than they'd ever be to a friend. You screw up and your internal voice goes: "You're such an idiot. Why do you always do this?"

Would you talk to a friend like that? Never. You'd say something like "Hey, that sucked, but everyone makes mistakes. What did you learn?"

Self-compassion isn't about lowering your standards or making excuses. It's about not being a jerk to yourself when things go wrong. Weirdly, the research shows this actually helps you improve faster than beating yourself up does.

Build a Life That Means Something to You

Carol Ryff spent decades studying what she calls eudaimonic wellbeing. Fancy Greek term for: happiness that comes from living according to your values, not just feeling good.

You can feel good eating pizza and watching Netflix. Nothing wrong with that. But lasting happiness comes from growth, purpose, autonomy. Doing things that align with what you actually care about.

I had to get honest about this. Was I doing things because I wanted to or because I thought I should? Big difference. Still figuring it out, but at least asking the question helps.

Your Brain Needs Other People

James Coan at UVA did these brain scan studies and found something interesting. When you're stressed and alone, your brain works really hard. When you're stressed and someone you trust is with you? Your brain literally calms down. Like measurably calms down.

We're not built to handle everything solo. Connection isn't just nice to have, it's how our nervous system regulates itself. This is why isolation feels so terrible. Your brain is basically in constant low-level stress mode.

Reach out to people more. Even just a text. Even just sitting in the same room. It matters.

Notice Good Moments While They're Happening

Robert Emmons and Sonja Lyubomirsky have done tons of research on gratitude. The finding: it works. People who practice gratitude are measurably happier.

But here's the thing I learned. It's not just about saying "thank you" or keeping a journal (though those help). It's about savoring. When something good happens, pause. Take it in. Let yourself feel it for more than two seconds before moving to the next thing.

We're so bad at this. Good thing happens, we immediately start worrying about the next thing or when it'll end. Try just...being in it for a minute.

Do Things Because You Want To

Deci and Ryan's Self-Determination Theory breaks down what creates lasting motivation: autonomy (you chose it), mastery (you're getting better), and connection (it matters to others too).

Goals that hit all three? Those stick. Goals you're doing because you "should" or someone else wants you to? Those drain you.

I started asking myself: do I actually want to do this, or do I think I'm supposed to want to do this? Game changer. Turns out I was pursuing a lot of stuff I didn't actually care about.

The New Car Stops Making You Happy

This is the one that really got me. Hedonic adaptation. Basically, you adapt to everything. New car, new apartment, new relationship, promotion. You get a happiness boost for like...a few weeks? Then you're back to baseline.

This is why chasing the next thing never works. You think "once I get X, I'll be happy." But you get X and you're the same person with the same brain that adapts to everything.

The solution isn't to stop wanting things. It's to build daily practices that create small moments of happiness. Those compound. The one-time highs don't.

So What Now?

Look, I'm not going to tell you to do all of this. That's overwhelming and you won't. Pick one thing. Maybe it's noticing good stuff at the end of the day. Maybe it's being less of a dick to yourself when you mess up.

The point is: happiness isn't random. It's not about getting lucky or having the perfect life. It's about small daily choices that add up over time. The research backs this up.

What Does It Take to Be a Good Friend?

By Punit Shah September 2025

We all want good friends. But what does it actually take to be one? Research from decades of psychology studies gives us clear answers. And the best part? It's simpler than you think.

What the Research Says

The Harvard Study of Adult Development spent 85+ years tracking what makes people happy. The answer? Good relationships. But here's what they also found: the quality of those relationships depends on how people show up for each other.

Being a good friend isn't about grand gestures or always knowing the right thing to say. It's about consistent, small acts of kindness and presence. It's about being reliable when it matters.

The Core Traits of Good Friends

1. They Show Up Consistently

Research by Dr. John Gottman on relationships found that the small moments matter more than the big ones. Good friends don't just appear during crises. They're there for the everyday stuff too.

A text to check in. Remembering what's going on in your life. Following up on things you mentioned. These small acts of showing up build trust over time.

How to do this:

  • Set reminders to check in with friends you haven't talked to in a while
  • Remember details they share and ask about them later
  • Show up for small things, not just emergencies
  • Be consistent, even when you're busy

2. They Listen Without Trying to Fix

UCLA research on emotional support found that people often just need to be heard, not given solutions. Good friends know when to listen and when to give advice.

Most of the time, people aren't looking for you to solve their problems. They want someone to understand what they're going through. That kind of validation is powerful.

How to do this:

  • Ask "Do you want advice or do you just need to vent?" before jumping in with solutions
  • Reflect back what you're hearing: "That sounds really frustrating"
  • Don't minimize their feelings or compare them to your own experiences
  • Give them space to fully express themselves before responding

3. They Celebrate Your Wins

Dr. Shelly Gable's research at UC Santa Barbara found that how you respond to good news matters more for relationship quality than how you respond to bad news. This is called "active constructive responding."

Good friends get genuinely excited when good things happen to you. They ask follow-up questions. They want details. They don't downplay your success or make it about themselves.

How to do this:

  • Match their energy when they share good news
  • Ask questions that let them relive the moment
  • Avoid responses like "That's great, but..." or immediately sharing your own story
  • Remember their wins and bring them up later

4. They're Honest, But Kind

Research from the Journal of Social and Personal Relationships shows that authenticity in friendships predicts satisfaction and longevity. But honesty without kindness damages relationships.

Good friends tell you the truth, even when it's hard. But they do it with care. They consider your feelings while still being real with you.

How to do this:

  • Ask permission before giving hard feedback: "Can I share something that might be tough to hear?"
  • Focus on behaviors, not character attacks
  • Lead with care: "I'm saying this because I care about you"
  • Be specific and constructive, not vague and critical

5. They Don't Keep Score

Studies on communal relationships (the kind where people give without expecting immediate return) show they're more satisfying and last longer than exchange-based relationships.

Good friends don't track who called last or who did more. They give freely, trusting it'll balance out over time. And when it doesn't? They still show up.

How to do this:

  • Reach out even if they didn't reach out to you last time
  • Be generous with your time and energy without expecting it back immediately
  • Remember that friendships go through seasons where one person needs more support
  • Let go of "fairness" in the short term; trust it balances over years

6. They Accept You As You Are

Carl Rogers' research on unconditional positive regard shows that feeling accepted for who you are (not who you could be) is fundamental to wellbeing.

Good friends don't try to change you or make you into their ideal version of you. They accept your quirks, your flaws, your weird interests. You don't have to perform for them.

How to do this:

  • Let friends be themselves without judgment
  • Don't try to "fix" their personality or lifestyle choices
  • Show interest in things that matter to them, even if you don't share the interest
  • Make them feel comfortable being authentic around you

7. They Make Time, Even When Busy

Research from the Journal of Social and Personal Relationships found that the biggest predictor of friendship quality is time spent together. Not money spent, not grand gestures. Just time.

Good friends prioritize making time, even when life gets hectic. They know that relationships need regular maintenance, not just occasional bursts of attention.

How to do this:

  • Schedule regular hangouts, even if they're brief
  • Don't wait until you have free time (you never will)
  • Meet friends where they are, do activities they enjoy
  • Quality matters, but so does quantity. Both are needed.

8. They're There in the Hard Times

Studies on social support show that having someone who shows up during difficult times reduces stress, speeds recovery, and improves overall health outcomes.

Good friends don't disappear when things get messy. They sit with you in the discomfort. They don't need you to be "fine" all the time.

How to do this:

  • Check in more frequently when friends are going through something hard
  • Offer specific help ("Can I bring you dinner Tuesday?") instead of vague offers ("Let me know if you need anything")
  • Don't avoid friends because you don't know what to say. Show up anyway.
  • Be comfortable with silence and difficult emotions

9. They Forgive and Move Forward

Research from Dr. Fred Luskin at Stanford shows that forgiveness benefits both parties. It lowers stress, reduces anger, and improves relationship satisfaction.

Good friends know that everyone messes up. They address issues directly, forgive, and don't hold grudges. They give second chances because they know they'll need them too.

How to do this:

  • Address issues when they happen instead of letting resentment build
  • Accept genuine apologies and move on
  • Don't bring up past mistakes during new conflicts
  • Remember that holding grudges hurts you more than them

10. They Practice Small Acts of Kindness

Research from Sonja Lyubomirsky at UC Riverside shows that performing acts of kindness increases happiness for both the giver and receiver. Small kindnesses strengthen bonds.

Good friends notice opportunities to be kind. They do small things that make life easier or brighter for the people they care about. No occasion needed.

How to do this:

  • Send a meme or article that made you think of them
  • Pick up their favorite snack when you're at the store
  • Leave encouraging notes or texts
  • Offer help before being asked
  • Celebrate small wins, not just big milestones

It's About Consistency, Not Perfection

Here's the truth: you don't need to do all of these things perfectly all the time. Good friends mess up. They forget to text back. They have their own lives and struggles.

What matters is the pattern. Do you generally show up? Are you mostly kind? Do you care about their wellbeing? That's what builds strong friendships.

The research is clear. Good friends aren't perfect. They're consistent. They're kind. They show up. And over time, those small acts compound into something meaningful.

Start Where You Are

Want to be a better friend? Pick one or two things from this list and focus on those. Maybe it's checking in more often. Maybe it's celebrating their wins better. Maybe it's just putting your phone away when you're together.

Small changes in how you show up can make a big difference. And the best part? When you become a better friend, you often end up with better friends too. People mirror the energy you bring to relationships.

Being a good friend is a practice, not a destination. You won't always get it right. But trying matters. Showing up matters. Kindness matters.
